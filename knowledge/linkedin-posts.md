## How to get hired from hackathons — entire Cal Hacks team hired at YC startup (NimbleRx)

**Hackathons aren't just for building. They're the fastest job search.**

Companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, Citadel, and FAANG all go to major hackathons. This is your quickest way to get your foot in the door — especially if you're underclassmen or don't have traditional big tech on your resume. 100x better than wasting time at career fairs.

**CAREER FAIR:** 30 seconds at booth, hand resume, ghosted.

**HACKATHON:** Sponsors have booths (same as career fairs). 36 hours to prove you can quickly ship their tools. Judges (who work there) watch you use their product.

Difference: One is words. One is proof.

**Framework:**

*Before the hackathon:*
1. Research sponsors (who's hiring?)
2. Pick sponsor tool to use strategically

*During the hackathon:*
1. Ask upfront at the booth if they're hiring, then connect on LinkedIn
2. Use their sponsor tools/APIs in your project
3. Demo to sponsors at booths

*After the hackathon:*
1. **POST ON LINKEDIN IMMEDIATELY**
2. Tag sponsors, judges, teammates

**Why:** NimbleRx wasn't even at Cal Hacks. They saw our LinkedIn post. Hired entire team. I've gotten interviews from other hackathons as well. Companies value these experiences because hackathons show you can hustle. Even if you don't get a job from the hackathon, you have something strong for your resume.

(Thank you NimbleRx for finding us and for all the lovely memories and free food in the summer.)

## What hackathon judges really care about (from a judge + MLH Top 50 winner)

**The best code doesn't win. The best experience does.**

As a former hackathon judge and winner of MLH Top 50 out of 150,000 hackers: the most technically advanced project doesn't win. The most memorable one does. Judges see 47 projects. By #30, their brain is on autopilot. Dashboard? Seen 12 times. Chat app? Seen 8. AI tool? Seen 15. Flashy projects break the pattern.

**Projects that really impressed me as a judge:**
- Take selfie → AI generates your cartoon doing parkour
- AR glasses overlaying real-time subtitles on people speaking
- Piano keys lighting up as AI generates melody

The difference? One you understand. One you experience. Think creatively. Stand out. Not better code. Better flash.

## 3-minute pitch tip that won Cal Hacks 1st overall

**I practiced the pitch 50+ times before judges even arrived.**

Most people rehearse to themselves at their table. I walked around the 2,500+ person venue and pitched to EVERY team during the waiting period after project submissions. They were busy and stressed. If they stopped to listen, I knew my pitch worked.

**3-minute template:**
1. **HOOK (0–10s):** Show, don't tell. Hand them something. Make them try it. No explanation yet.
2. **EXPERIENCE (10–30s):** Let them experience it first before you bombard them with details.
3. **MESSAGE (30s–3min):** Summarize it in one sentence, then explain.

Practice by walking to every table. Every team, adjust ONE thing. Watch when they stop, zone out, or repeat your words. Track what works: Iterations 1–10 (explanation first) → "OK". Iterations 20–35 (experience first) → "Wait, WHAT?!" By the time you present to judges, you already know your pitch works.

**Key lesson:** Athletes don't compete cold; they warm up first. All winners need to warm up. So warm up your pitch.

## Pitch before code — how we won Cal Hacks 1st after losing 15+ hackathons

**I wrote the pitch before writing any code.**

Won Cal Hacks 1st Overall (world's largest collegiate hackathon, 2,500+ hackers, $100k+ prize pool) after losing 15+ hackathons. What changed?

When you write your pitch first, you're forced to answer: WHO has this problem? WHY does it matter to them? WHAT proves you solved it? Your product becomes proof of your pitch.

**Our Cal Hacks pitch:** "What if music adapted to your emotions in real-time using your brainwaves?"
- WHO: Everyone (emotion + music = universal)
- WHY: Music doesn't currently adapt to how you feel
- WHAT: Let judges experience it themselves

Hours 2–35: Built an EEG headset that changes music based on brainwaves. Skipped UI polish and extra features. Result: Judges put on the headset. Music changed instantly with their emotions. They didn't just see it, they felt it.

This psychology applies everywhere. Apple tilts screens at 76° so you touch them in-store. We did the same at Cal Hacks.
